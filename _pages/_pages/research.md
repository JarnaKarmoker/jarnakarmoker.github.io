My research examines the molecular regulation of Schwann cell function in the peripheral nervous system. In Dr. Guolei Zhou’s laboratory at Arkansas State University, I investigate how Cyclase-Associated Protein 1 (CAP1) regulates important Schwann cell processes, including adhesion, migration, proliferation, and signaling responses mediated by cyclic AMP (cAMP).

## Current Thesis Project
**Investigating CAP1 (Cyclase-Associated Protein 1) functions in Schwann cells and their regulation by cyclic AMP**

The overarching goal of my M.S. research is to define the role of CAP1 signaling in Schwann cells and determine how this pathway may contribute to peripheral nerve injury repair. Using RNA interference (RNAi)-based CAP1 knockdown, I have observed substantial changes in Rap1, ERK, and cofilin activity, along with associated effects on Schwann cell functional behavior.

I have also found that activation of cAMP signaling induces CAP1 dephosphorylation and is associated with activation of Rap1 and ERK signaling in Schwann cells. These findings support a working model in which CAP1 functions within cAMP-responsive signaling networks to regulate important cellular events relevant to peripheral nerve repair.

To extend this work into more physiologically relevant systems, I am also pursuing neuron–Schwann cell co-culture approaches and exploring future directions related to nerve injury models.

## Why This Research Matters
Peripheral nerve injury and peripheral neuropathies remain major biomedical challenges. Schwann cells are central to peripheral nerve repair, regeneration, and myelination. Mechanistic insight into CAP1-dependent signaling in Schwann cells may help identify new therapeutic targets for nerve injury repair, peripheral neuropathies, and demyelinating disease.
